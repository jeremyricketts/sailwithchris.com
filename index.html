Vava'u approach

Current position:
18.60S by 173.85W
See A Map

08 September 2012 by the crew of the Privateer.

Approaching Neiafu in the Vava’u group of Tonga is very straight forward. This seems to be the first place most cruising boats decide to check in before they explore the rest of the Tongan islands, so the main harbor of Neiafu is well charted and marked. Approaching from the north east of the main island, the only real hazard to worry about is yup, you guessed it, an FAD. I did spot the one about 5 miles north of the light tower, and though I did not check my GPS at the time, the coordinates of, 18deg32.7S-174deg04.2W, seemed very accurate. Once in the lee of Vava’u near the light tower entrace, the islands are not quite high enough to completely block the trade winds, so sailing all the way to the main harbor is possible if you so decide. We sailed most of the way in to the lighted range markers, and I think you will want to also when you quickly remember how fun it is sailing at hull speed through all these beautiful islands in lake like conditions. Once in sight of the first range markers a heading of 65.5 degrees true will bring you in safely past the two green can buoys off to your starboard side. I mainly use my eyeballs, but if you use CM93 charts in coordination with a GPS unit, it has been reported to be quite a bit off. There is one discrepancy I found on the CM93 charts. After the first green can buoy, the electronic charts say that the buoy marking shoal water off to your starboard side is black, but it is instead green. There are also two red can buoys off to your port as well that are not stated on the CM93 charts while on the entrance bearing. There is a second set of range markers after you round the second green buoy, but they will be astern as they are the exit bearings for leaving the harbor. The chart says 291 degrees true, but the correct bearing upon entering will be 111 degrees true, once you glance behind you and line them up astern. If you’re like me you will surprised at how many boats are moored in the harbor. There are plenty of moorings available from many of the private companies located in Neiafu, many of which monitor channel 16. Be aware though, you will at some point have to dock your boat on one of the two wharfs for the authorities to board your boat and check you in, so you may want to take care of that first and then worry about where to settle in. There are a few anchorages inside Neiafu, but we decided to go around the corner and anchor in the lee of Kafoa which is anchorage #4 on the Moorings map that you can purchase from the Moorings company above the Aquarium cafe, and it was extremely protected.
Formalities:
It can get quite a bit busy as they have a few boats checking in/out daily. There are two main docks to tie up to at the wharf. One is much bigger than the other with permanent black fenders, but for boats with lower free board your best bet is probably the smaller one. There are four separate officials who will board your boat and ask you to fill out the normal clearance papers. They really only come aboard to have you fill out the necessary paper work and chat a bit, and at no point did they do a thorough search of the vessel and start digging around the boat for vegetables, produce and the like. The process can take a few hours especially if you get there between 12:30 and 1:30 as this is their lunch hour. Health is $100 Pa’anga(about $55 US), Quarantine is $30 Pa’anga, and a 30 day visa is free for the first 30 days. After that, it is $69 Pa’anga a month per person.

Vaikona Chasm

Current position:
19.0633 by 169.8967
See A Map

12 August 2012 by the crew of the Privateer.

Vaikona chasm/underwater caves: This place was the highlight of our trip. On the map from the visitors center it says that this hike is guided only, but we and a few other cruisers decided to go anyway. The trail is well marked and is pretty easy until you get near the water’s edge. You will then see an old sign that says, “Vaikona chasm: Falling rocks cave unsafe.” PERFECT!!! There are sturdy ropes set up that descend into the chasm, but we brought a 50 foot line as backup just to be safe. The boulders are extremely slippery deeper in the chasm as you descend so wear some good kicks with traction. There is a challenging bit descending down one boulder and committing to let go of the rope and shift your weight onto a lower one over a drop off, but the crisp, electric blue of the chasms fresh water pond once at the bottom is so worth it! When the afternoon sun beams through the large rift at the surface, bathing in the pool is absolutely amazing! If you’re a bit more daring, and you have a good underwater torch, there is a maze of underwater caves that start at the far end of the pool. We made our way as deep as we could into five different caves, some only a few feet above the surface. The best of them all is swimming back under the first arch on the return back to the chasm, the sunlight dances on the surface and gets brighter and brighter until you finally reach the surface.

Niue Approach

Current position:
19.0633S by 169.8967W
See A Map

10 August 2012 by the crew of the Privateer.

Niue(‘new-ay’) is one of the smallest nations in the Pacific. It is unlike most other Pacific islands in that it was thrust up and out of the ocean long ago and is now a completely ‘raised’ coral island. The abundance of underwater coral, lack of sand, and the sudden drop off into deep water surrounding Niue makes anchoring extremely difficult. The approach to Niue is pretty straight forward. There are no real hazards to worry about other than some FAD’s(that are unlit) put in by the local fisherman. There are many around the entire island, and some close to Alofi, but none seem to be more than a mile or so offshore. The chart has an entrance bearing of 123degrees true into Alofi, but this can be altered as long as you steer clear of the outlying reefs and fishing buoys. Once in sight of the Alofi wharf, the mooring field is to the south. The mooring balls are bright orange and have reflective tape if you decide to make landfall by night. The Niue Yacht Club, which might be the best little yacht club in the world, has about 20 regularly maintained moorings in Alofi. Most of the moorings are two 1 ton concrete blocks chained together and all the gear attached to the blocks are proper mooring shackles, pendants, and oversized three strand nylon line. The fee is $15 NZ(about $13US) a night and for every 6 days, the 7th is free. There are two wash rooms and showers with pressure hot and cold water a short walk up the dock and the yacht club in town has a book library, plenty of large chart tables, FREE internet, and cold beer and wine! All this for only $78US a week, and you’re backyard has some of the best diving and hiking in the world!

Formalities:
Both the yacht club and Niue radio station monitor channel 16. Once in radio range you can radio ahead to the yacht club to see if there are any available moorings. August, September and October are Niue’s busy months, and with all the puddle jumpers making their way west, there may be a chance they are full. There is a large mooring always left open for the cargo ship from New Zealand, and the yacht club allowed a boat to use that mooring until one of the regular ones was clear, so there are some options if the mooring field is full. Let Niue station know your ETA of arrival as well, and notify them when you have tied up securely to a mooring. They will then set up a time for you to meet with the customs officials on the wharf. When taking your skiff to the wharf you’ll notice that no boats are left side tied to the wall, and if you do you will cause yourself, the rest of the anchorage, and the local fisherman a lot of grief. There is a crane on the south side and you are required to hoist your skiff out of the water every time you come and go. The crane will lift small fishing boats and there is a dinghy dolly, so moving even the largest of cruising skiffs is not a problem. Make sure to give way to any locals coming in and out of the water first and always swing the crane arm back over the water for the next person. Keith from the yacht club will most likely meet you at the dock and show you around after you meet with customs. Leave any trash on your boat for a later day as they will try to stick you with a trash collecting fee for the first day. Once clear with customs walk up the road from the wharf and turn right to the police station with your passport. You’ll get your stamp and a fee of $34NZ per person is paid upon departure. Now it’s off to the yacht club, which is south of the police station below the backpackers hostel, for a cold beer!

A'sua trail (Massacre Bay)

Current position:
14.27S by 170.68W
See A Map

26 July 2012 by the crew of the Privateer.

Another day hike! This hike is only for the adventurous. Take the bus towards Leone and tell the driver you would like to go to Pava’ia’i. Once off the bus, wait for a bus going up the hill A’oloaufou or just hitch, which worked fine for us. At the top of the hill keep your eye out for a big church with a grass area out front. There is a small, paved road off the right that works its way down towards the trail head. You will pass a big piece of property with cattle and pigs and then make your way up a small hill where there are two houses and a sign that says ‘PRIVATE PROPERTY’ Beware the first house has aggressive dogs, even the little lap dog. Having your bag ready to swing and a rock ready to throw is not a bad idea. Yelling ‘ALU!’, which means go away in Samoan, seams to work fine, but just be prepared. At the last house, ask for permission to pass and tell them that you want to go down to massacre bay. They are very friendly and bringing them back a few papayas is not a bad idea. the trail is single track and bush, and if it rains, which it most likely will, it will be nice and muddy! It is a 2.5m hike to the beach. The last part of the trail is very steep, so grabbing a downed tree branch for a walking stick helps a lot. the trail then opens up to a fruit and vegetable patch and over a little fresh water river and into the sand. An extremely nice couple, Jim and Maria, are full time residents and have been for two and a half years. They are the only people who live in Massacre Bay, as the last family to leave was some time after the tsunami. Jim is very knowledgeable about the history of the bay and will give you a great tour. They invited us to stay for dinner, but since we had to get back to the top of the trail to catch a bus before night fall, Maria made us a dinner to go of lobster and steamed green bananas! The last bus going down the hill leaves around 4 o’clock so make sure and give yourself enough time to hike out. We visited with Jim and Maria for too long and missed the last bus, but hitching back down was not a problem. Exploring the more remote areas in American Samoa is well worth it. So many sailors drop the hook in Pago Pago, grab a big mac, pick up their parts at the post office, and then sail out a few days later and make a broad generalization that the whole island is extremely dirty. Get off the beaten path here, and the people, their families, and the land is really very beautiful.

Fagatele Bay

Current position:
14.27S by 170.68W
See A Map

22 July 2012 by the crew of the Privateer.

We took a break from working on the boat today and went on a day hike at Fagatele Bay. Leaving from the Pago anchorage you can jump on a Leone bus and let the driver know you want to go to go to the US mart. There is a street that heads down toward the water just before the US mart, so just start walking. There is trash everywhere on this road as there is a dump site not to far down, but I assure you once you reach the trail head it is worth it. The road comes to a gate and there is a house with a large yard with a tree in the center. Ask them for permission to use the trails and they will have you sign a piece of paper with your name and where you are from. Once on the trail head, it is a night and day difference from the anchorage. Getting a copy of the national park trail map from the visitors center is not a bad idea either. The visitor’s center is located across from the filling station on the south side of the anchorage. There are a few trails to choose from. The trail down to Fagatele bay is well maintained and is a very easy in and out hike. There are stairs at the end of the trail which lead down to the sand. Keep your eye out for buoys and floats washed up on the beach, I found a few and brought them back to the boat. If it has rained a few days prior, there will be some nice fresh water showers against the huge mossy rocks. The bay is a marine preserve so the wildlife and snorkeling is great.

Reusing Sta-lok swedge fittings

Current position:
14.27S by 170.68W
See A Map

15 July 2012 by the crew of the Privateer.

We are tied up to the inter-island ferry dock which is located next to the marine police station. The concrete dock has huge rubber fenders, so if you’re worried about your gel coat, BYO fenders and lots of them. The area is huge and flat which is perfect for rigging work. The head stay came down no problem, but the trick was how to get the new wire rope to feed through the roller furling foil pieces. If you have any kind of roller furling head sail on your boat, it is probably like mine, and the foil pieces are aluminum and all the set screws used to attach these foil pieces together are stainless. My ProFurl unit is pretty old so these set screws were not coming out even after using penetrating lube and heat. A much easier way is to make sure to leave the old wire in and carefully epoxy the new wire to the old, making sure to keep a similar diameter. Once the epoxy is nice and set the next day, just pull on the old wire and bada bing, you now have a perfectly fed new wire in your foil! The new Sta-Lok fittings are a breeze to use, though the new packaging does not have detailed instructions on specific torque, etc. there are plenty of videos on you tube going through the process in detail. Reusing the fittings that were already on the boat is another story. Most riggers will tell you that bedding these type of swedge fittings with 3M 5200 is a must. I don’t know how many of these riggers have ever reused these fittings before, which the manufacture says is fine, but 5200 is great for bulkheads, thru hull joints, hull damage, but in my humble opinion,it has no place inside of a swedge fitting. Any type of silicon or less permanent adhesive like 3M 4000 will keep the water out just fine. It took a whole day just to clean out the old 5200 from the fittings. Soaking them in turpentine or mineral spirits overnight will make the process go a lot faster

Pago Pago, American Samoa

Current position:
14.27S by 170.68W
See A Map

05 July 2012 by the crew of the Privateer.

Pago Pago(pronounced, “Pang-go Pang-go”) is located on the island of Tutuila, American Somoa’s largest island. Approaching Pago from the north east you are advised to keep a look out for FAD’s and fishing rafts, most of which are stated on the charts to be unlit. My chart, dated from the 1960’s, had two FAD’s and two rafts, but I later looked at another cruisers chart which was from the 1980’s and a few of the rafts and FAD’s were crossed off, and it looked to be an original marking on the chart itself. Pretty much what I’m saying, is I have no clue where these things are, if they are regularly maintained, or if they even exist at all, so use caution.

The entrance to Pago is very straight forward as this is a heavily traveled, commercial shipping port. I slowed down the day before arriving to Pago, so as to make landfall during the day, but after seeing how well lit the bouys are, how wide the entrance is, and that there are lighted range markers, I would make this port in darkness with no hesitation. You are required to notify the Port Captain of your arrival on ch. 16, though I was unsuccessful upon my arrival and was helped out by a woman named Judy on ‘Windrider’, and she explained which formalities I needed to take care of and where to go. She actually has a sheet printed out on the basic formalities and some local info on the town itself which she hands out to cruisers whenever she can and it was pretty helpful. Anchoring here is not nearly as bad as all the horror stories I have read about. It did take a few failed attempts at getting the hook set, but once she was in she didn’t move an inch. There are several ‘bumps’ scattered around the anchorage, one of which is to the SW of the big, orange quarantine mooring. These bumps are much shallower than the rest of the anchorage which averages 35 feet. I dropped the hook on a 20 foot bump and had much better luck with holding power. It was either luck or maybe that not as much debris has settled on these bumps, which gives your hook a better opportunity at settling into the muddy ground, but either way it gives your anchor scope a better advantage. Once you’re all settled, you can take your skiff to the floating docks in front of McDonald’s or there is a very small, rocky beach with a nice tree trunk to tie up to right next to the gas station on the south side. This is a much closer option if you are like me and only have a rowing dinghy. Make sure to tie up good because high tide will float your dinghy unless you bring it up onto the grass.

Formalities: First you have to check in with the Port Captain, Salila. His office is located on top of the harbor facility building which if you head east from McDonald’s it will be on your left hand side where all the big containers are stacked on top of each other. The guards at the entrance gate will tell you where his office is. Then it’s off to customs, which is in the same building on the first floor. After checking in with both the port captain and customs you have to walk a bit farther to reach the immigration office. Go back out to the gate and turn left. You will pass Sadie’s by the sea restaurant, which has happy hour daily, from 4-6 p.m. The burger there is excellent and it’s one of the few restaurants that serve the local beer, Vailima on draft. You’ll then pass the high school on your right side and then the Bank of Hawaii. Turn right into the driveway after the B of H and there will be a brown government building behind it. Immigration is on the first floor, and the AC in the office feels SO good! Now you’re checked in. The fee is $170 US for the boat, once you leave.
